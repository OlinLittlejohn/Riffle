Oregon River 🐟 Species

Common sportfish found in Oregon's rivers and waterways.

🐟 Add images/chinook-salmon.jpg
Chinook Salmon
Oncorhynchus tshawytscha
📅 Spring & Fall 📍 Willamette, Rogue, Deschutes

Oregon's most prized sportfish with two distinct runs — spring Chinook (March–June) and fall Chinook (August–November). The Willamette and Rogue rivers are top destinations. Spring fish are considered superior table fare.

🐟 Add images/coho-salmon.jpg
Coho Salmon
Oncorhynchus kisutch
📅 Fall 📍 Siletz, Alsea, Nestucca

Fall run fish (September–December) found primarily in coastal rivers. The Siletz and Alsea rivers offer some of Oregon's best Coho fishing. Known for aggressive strikes on spinners and spoons.

🐟 Add images/winter-steelhead.jpg
Winter Steelhead
Oncorhynchus mykiss
📅 Winter & Early Spring 📍 Sandy, Clackamas, Rogue

Oregon's iconic sea-run rainbow trout returns to rivers December–April. The Sandy and Clackamas rivers near Portland see heavy runs. Large, powerful fish averaging 8–12 lbs.

🐟 Add images/summer-steelhead.jpg
Summer Steelhead
Oncorhynchus mykiss
📅 Summer & Fall 📍 Deschutes, North Umpqua

Enter rivers June–October while flows are low and clear. The Deschutes River below Pelton Dam is legendary for summer steelhead on dry flies. The North Umpqua is considered one of the finest summer steelhead rivers in the world.

🐟 Add images/rainbow-trout.jpg
Rainbow Trout
Oncorhynchus mykiss
📅 Year-round 📍 McKenzie, Metolius, Deschutes

Found statewide in cold, clear streams year-round. The McKenzie River is famous for its wild rainbow trout and traditional drift boat fishing. The Metolius River near Camp Sherman holds trophy-sized fish.

🐟 Add images/cutthroat-trout.jpg
Cutthroat Trout
Oncorhynchus clarkii
📅 Spring & Summer 📍 Illinois, Coquille, Nehalem

Oregon's native trout in two forms — coastal cutthroat in coastal rivers (spring–summer) and westslope cutthroat in eastern mountain streams. The Illinois River in SW Oregon offers excellent wild cutthroat fishing.

🐟 Add images/bull-trout.jpg
Bull Trout
Salvelinus confluentus
📅 Summer & Fall 📍 Metolius, Lostine, Imnaha

Native to Oregon's coldest headwater streams, most active June–October. The Metolius River holds a strong population. Listed as threatened — catch and release only in most Oregon waters.

🐟 Add images/brown-trout.jpg
Brown Trout
Salmo trutta
📅 Fall 📍 Deschutes, Metolius

Thrives in the Deschutes and Metolius rivers. Fall (September–November) is prime time when browns are aggressive ahead of spawning. Known for being selective — a true test for fly anglers.

🐟 Add images/smallmouth-bass.jpg
Smallmouth Bass
Micropterus dolomieu
📅 Summer 📍 John Day, Umpqua, Rogue

Thrives in warm, rocky river sections during summer (June–September). The John Day River offers some of the best smallmouth bass fishing in the Pacific Northwest. Puts up an incredible fight.

🐟 Add images/walleye.jpg
Walleye
Sander vitreus
📅 Spring & Winter 📍 Columbia River, Snake River

Found in the Columbia and Snake rivers with peak fishing in spring (March–May) and winter. The Columbia River near John Day Dam is a consistently productive walleye destination.

🐟 Add images/channel-catfish.jpg
Channel Catfish
Ictalurus punctatus
📅 Summer 📍 Willamette, Columbia, Snake

Most active during warm summer months (June–August) in slower river sections. Night fishing with cut bait near deep holes is most productive on the lower Willamette and Columbia rivers.

🐟 Add images/white-sturgeon.jpg
White Sturgeon
Acipenser transmontanus
📅 Year-round 📍 Columbia River, Snake River

Ancient species that can live 100+ years and grow over 10 feet long. Year-round in the Columbia and Snake rivers. Strict slot size limits apply — most fish must be released. A true bucket-list catch.
